Most analytics stacks look fine until you actually look at them. GA4 will show you events and conversions and pretty trend lines, but it won't tell you that `add_to_cart` has been firing zero times for four days because a CSS selector broke after a PDP redesign. It won't tell you that 47% of your purchase events are missing `transaction_id` because a dataLayer variable returns undefined on Recharge subscription orders. It just quietly reports whatever it has.

Analytics Audit was built to fill that gap. It connects to your GTM container, GA4 property, and BigQuery export — all read-only, through your own Google account — and runs a structured set of checks that produce actual findings: what's broken, how bad it is, and what to fix. Critical means it's blocking revenue measurement. Warning means data quality is at risk. It reads like a code review, not a dashboard.

The BigQuery module is where it gets interesting. Seven SQL checks run directly against your raw GA4 export — purchase funnel conversion rates, parameter completeness on transactions, duplicate event schemas, session attribution gaps, daily volume outages on critical events. These are things that are completely invisible from the GA4 UI, which works off processed data that's already had the interesting bits aggregated away. If 30% of your sessions have a null source/medium, GA4 will happily attribute that traffic to Direct and never mention it.

The whole thing is a single Express server with no build step, no database, and no stored data. OAuth tokens live in memory for one session and expire after an hour. Your clients' data never touches anything you don't own.
